\section{Introduction}

Particle-based simulation methodologies represent physical systems as collections of discrete interacting entities. 
The evolution of these systems is governed by interaction rules that determine how particles influence one another through
 time. In existing particle methods, these interactions are commonly derived from constitutive relationships, equations of 
state, potential-energy functions, collision-response models, or other phenomenon-specific formulations. Although the 
mathematical forms of these models differ substantially, they generally share a common objective: the generation of 
forces that are subsequently integrated to produce particle motion and the evolving state of the system.

Because particle interactions determine both the physical behavior and computational characteristics of a simulation, the formulation of the 
interaction model occupies a central role within particle methodologies. Different assumptions regarding how particles interact may 
lead to substantially different system behavior, numerical properties, and computational requirements.

The present work is motivated by the observation that physical interactions occur locally and instantaneously. 
An interacting particle responds only to quantities available within its immediate state and environment and possesses no direct 
knowledge of previous or future states. The particle does not know the duration or eventual outcome of an interaction, 
nor does it possess knowledge of the physical identity of the object with which it interacts. Whether the neighboring 
object is another particle, a boundary, a solid surface, or some other physical entity is an interpretation made by the 
observer rather than by the interacting particle itself. Interaction therefore occurs exclusively through quantities 
available at the present instant. 


The Field Particle Method (FPM) investigates the consequences of adopting this present-state interpretation by restricting 
the governing dynamics to quantities locally accessible at the current instant. Geometry, force, acceleration, 
and momentum are therefore treated as dynamically fundamental quantities, while quantities requiring integration over 
time, distance, or system history are regarded as observer-derived diagnostics.
