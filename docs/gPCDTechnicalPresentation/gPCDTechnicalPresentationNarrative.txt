SLIDE 2:
Before discussing the technology, it's important to understand why particle simulation matters.
Particle-based simulation is already used to solve important problems across a wide range of industries. In energy and infrastructure, it helps engineers design and optimize systems involving fluids, materials, and complex physical interactions. In advanced manufacturing, it is used to model powders, materials processing, and next-generation production techniques. And in emerging AI and digital twin applications, simulation helps create realistic virtual environments for prediction, optimization, and decision-making.
These are not future applications. They are important industries today. As organizations seek more accurate models and more realistic simulations, the demand for computational capability continues to grow.
Particle simulation is already creating value across critical sectors of the economy, making it both an important technical challenge and a significant commercial opportunity.
SLIDE 3
As particle simulations grow, one task increasingly dominates the computational workload: determining which particles are interacting with each other.
This process, called collision detection, is performed continuously throughout a simulation and is essential to modeling physical behavior.
The more particles we add, the more potential interactions must be evaluated. As a result, computing requirements can grow rapidly, driving up cost and limiting the size of simulations that can be performed efficiently.
Solving the collision detection problem is therefore one of the most important challenges in large-scale particle simulation.

SLIDE 5
gPCD is a proprietary GPU-native collision detection architecture that adapts collision detection to the way GPUs naturally process graphics.
Rather than forcing the GPU to adapt to collision detection, gPCD adapts collision detection to the way GPUs naturally process graphics.
This fundamentally different approach allows us to harness one of the world's most powerful parallel processing technologies in a novel way to efficiently identify particle interactions on a single GPU.
By aligning the collision detection process with the strengths of modern graphics hardware, gPCD can perform large-scale particle interaction analysis without requiring increasingly large and costly computing platforms.
The result is a collision detection framework designed specifically for modern GPU architectures, enabling larger and more realistic simulations using commodity hardware.

SLIDE  6
A new technology only matters if it delivers measurable results.
To validate the gPCD architecture, we tested the framework using more than 11 million particles on a single GPU. The system successfully processed over 131 million particle interactions per second while maintaining exact collision detection.
The significance of these results is not simply speed. The significance is that they demonstrate the ability to perform large-scale particle simulation using commodity hardware rather than increasingly large and expensive computing platforms.
These results provide evidence that the underlying architecture works as intended and can support the computational demands of next-generation particle simulation.

SLIDE 8

There are many approaches to collision detection and particle simulation, each developed to address different requirements and constraints.
Some solutions prioritize accuracy but require significant computing resources. Others prioritize speed but rely on approximations. Many were designed before modern GPUs became the powerful parallel processing platforms they are today.
gPCD takes a different approach. By adapting collision detection to the way GPUs naturally process graphics, it combines exact collision detection with GPU-native execution on commodity hardware.
Our goal is not to replace every existing solution. Our goal is to occupy a unique position where accuracy, performance, and accessibility can coexist within a single framework.
This positioning creates opportunities to complement, enhance, or integrate with a wide range of existing simulation platforms.
