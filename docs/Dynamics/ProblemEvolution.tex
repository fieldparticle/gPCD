\section{Problem Evolution}

\subsection{Source-Local Area Weighting Breaks Conservation}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The unweighted geometric pair impulse was:

\begin{equation}
J_{ij}=q_{ij}A_{ij}\Delta t.
\end{equation}

Because pair overlap area and pair stiffness are symmetric,
\(A_{ij}=A_{ji}\) and \(q_{ij}=q_{ji}\), independently evaluated source and
target impulses have equal scalar magnitude.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

The failed weighted form applied a source-local overlap-area weight to the raw
impulse:

\begin{equation}
J_{ij}
=
q_{ij}A_{ij}
\left(
\frac{A_{ij}}{\sum_k A_{ik}}
\right)
\Delta t.
\end{equation}

The denominator is the total overlap area for the current source.  When the two
particles have different surrounding contact sets:

\begin{equation}
\sum_k A_{ik}\ne\sum_k A_{jk},
\end{equation}

and therefore:

\begin{equation}
J_{ij}\ne J_{ji}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

No solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The source-owned execution structure remains valid: each source reads the same
frame-start snapshot, vector-sums its contact contributions, and writes only
its own velocity.  The conservation failure is in the source-local impulse
magnitude.  Different source contexts calculate different magnitudes for the
same pair contact, so the independently applied impulses are not
equal-and-opposite.

The current \texttt{ThreeParticle} weighted capture ends with:

\begin{verbatim}
vector momentum drift = approximately 0.04899135
kinetic-energy drift  = +0.00090284
internal momentum     = 0
\end{verbatim}

The internal-momentum ledgers drain completely, and accumulated compression
and release impulse balance, but whole-system vector momentum and kinetic
energy do not return to their starting values.  Complete scalar ledger return
therefore does not establish conservation.

Source-local area weights may describe a source allocation, but multiplying
them into the physical pair impulse breaks pair compatibility.  Removing that
factor restored equal-and-opposite pair evaluation and exact vector momentum
in the \texttt{ThreeParticle} test.  Kinetic-energy drift remained, motivating
the separate central-force model.

\subsection{Overlap Area as a Central Force Field}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

\texttt{WeightedDynamics} stores compression impulse in source-owned contact
ledgers and returns that scalar impulse during separation.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

\texttt{ForceDynamics} applies the central pair force
\(F_{ij}=q_{ij}A_{ij}\) during every overlapping timestep.  It stores no
contact momentum and uses
\(U_{ij}(d)=q_{ij}\int_d^{r_i+r_j}A_{ij}(s)\,ds\) for energy diagnostics.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Particle solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The force is symmetric for both independently evaluated sources, so pair
impulses are equal-and-opposite.  The changing contact-normal direction is
handled directly by the central force rather than by returning a stored scalar
impulse along a later normal.

At \(\Delta t=0.05\), the current \texttt{ThreeParticle} capture ends with:

\begin{verbatim}
vector momentum drift = 0
total-energy drift    = approximately -0.00000017
potential energy      = 0
\end{verbatim}

Equal-time timestep refinement drives the completed-collision energy error
toward zero.  The current \texttt{ForceDynamics} implementation immediately
accumulates contact forces into a source-local \texttt{totalForce}, updates
velocity once, and then updates position with semi-implicit Euler.  Vector
momentum remains conserved, while integration produces a small bounded energy
excursion.

\subsection{Statistical Energy Validation Requires Walls}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

Two- and three-particle tests measured bounded energy excursions and small
completed-collision residuals.  They established timestep convergence and
showed that completed-contact force-work residual equals final total-energy
residual, but they do not establish the long-term statistical behavior of many
collisions.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

A long-running particles-in-a-box test now exercises many elastic particle and
wall collisions.  It measures whether the numerical energy excursion remains
bounded or accumulates into systematic drift.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Particle solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

Walls are now stationary equal-particle ghosts processed by a direct
four-wall scan.  Their conservative overlap-derived force and potential
participate in the same source-local dynamics and energy diagnostics as
particle contacts.

In the current 10,000-step \texttt{ParticlesInBox} capture, total-energy error
rises and falls between approximately \(-1.85\times10^{-5}\) and
\(+1.90\times10^{-5}\), with a final error of approximately
\(-7.34\times10^{-7}\).  The result is bounded over this run rather than
monotonic, enabling continued statistical validation over longer runs and
multiple random seeds.

\subsection{Return ForceDynamics to One Source Invocation}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

\texttt{ForceDynamics} used velocity Verlet, requiring a frame-start force
scan, predicted positions, a second force scan, and stored current and
predicted accelerations.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

Each source invocation now detects particle and wall contacts once,
immediately accumulates their forces into source-local \texttt{totalForce},
calls \texttt{CalcVelocity}, and then calls \texttt{CalcPosition}.  Contact
slots remain available for diagnostics but are not force inputs.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The current structure maps directly to a GLSL compute invocation without a
global synchronization point or second force scan.  It preserves the central
overlap-area force law, source ownership, and equal-and-opposite independent
particle-pair evaluation.  Semi-implicit Euler has a larger instantaneous
excursion than velocity Verlet in small collision tests, but the current
\texttt{ParticlesInBox} result is bounded and performs better than the Verlet
implementation in that long-running test.

\subsection{ForceDynamics GLSL Export Reaches Vulkan Runtime}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

Python \texttt{ForceDynamics} was shaped for GLSL, but the generated
\texttt{ForceDynamics.glsl} path was still a compile and integration
experiment.  The export path had to settle forward declarations, compute
atomic error reporting, ForceDynamics-owned constants, the wall-contact
wrapper contract, and application-generated boundary configuration.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

Generated \texttt{ForceDynamics.glsl} now runs through the Vulkan compute path
for the ordinary scenarios tested so far.  The permanent compute shader owns
the particle cell scan and directly scans the four configured walls.  The
generated dynamics include owns ForceDynamics constants, source-local force
accumulation, ordinary particle and wall geometry, velocity update, and
position update.  Wall dimensions and \texttt{wall\_contact\_offset} remain
application-generated in \texttt{boundary.glsl}.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Particle solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The ordinary ForceDynamics path has crossed from Python-only validation into
Vulkan runtime validation.  The working path includes ordinary particle
contacts, ordinary wall contacts, direct four-wall processing, source-local
\texttt{totalForce}, \texttt{CalcVelocity}, and \texttt{CalcPosition}.

\texttt{ResolveStartingCollision} is intentionally not part of this completed
validation.  Starting collisions require persistent starting-contact state,
and that state still needs a GLSL buffer/export implementation through
\texttt{StartingContactState.glsl}.  Until that path is implemented, the
export result is a particle solution for ordinary particle and wall dynamics,
with starting-overlap resolution deferred.

\subsection{Retire MomentumDynamics}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

\texttt{MomentumDynamics} remained registered as a selectable runtime model
without an active model source document.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

\texttt{MomentumDynamics} is removed from the active runtime registry.  Its
model-specific Python files are retained under
\texttt{python/base/old/MomentumDynamics}.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The active selectable models are now \texttt{ForceDynamics} and
\texttt{VerAForceDynamics}, and both have corresponding source documents
included by \texttt{main.tex}.  \texttt{VerAForceDynamics} is an independently
wired baseline copy used as the shadow model before changes are made to the
live implementation.  Retiring rather than deleting old models preserves
their implementation history without presenting them as maintained.

\subsection{Resolve Starting Overlap Without Extra Rebound}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

\texttt{ForceDynamics} treated a starting overlap exactly like any later
physical overlap.  The physical radii defined both the force magnitude and the
diagnostic potential energy from the first frame.  In
\texttt{ResolveStartingCollisions}, this meant the initial configuration
stored a large physical-overlap potential and produced a stronger rebound than
the same collision would have produced if it had evolved into that overlap
from first contact.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

Live \texttt{ForceDynamics} records contacts that are already overlapping at
initialization.  For each starting particle or wall contact, the starting
center distance becomes an effective separation limit.  The force and
potential-energy diagnostics use the effective geometry while the starting
contact is active, so the initial configured overlap contributes no force
until the contact compresses deeper than the starting separation.

When the contact rebounds back to its starting separation, the starting contact
is marked resolved.  Physical-radius force is then suppressed until the actual
physical overlap clears, preventing a second rebound from the configured
overlap.  \texttt{VerAForceDynamics} remains the shadow baseline that uses the
previous physical-radius behavior.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Particle solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The fix keeps normal contacts unchanged: non-starting particle and wall
scenarios remain physically identical to the VerA baseline.  Starting-overlap
scenarios intentionally diverge from VerA because live ForceDynamics removes
the initial artificial stored potential.  Current targeted runs show
\texttt{ResolveStartingCollisions}, \texttt{ResolveStartingWallCollision}, and
\texttt{ResolveStartingWPCollision} completing without runtime errors and
draining their starting-contact state.

The result is local to starting contacts and avoids an external evaluator.  It
does introduce persistent contact state, so future GLSL work must carry that
state outside the transient per-frame contact slots.  The result is still
marked a particle solution because broader multi-contact starting-overlap
validation remains future work.

\subsection{Reservoir Flow Uses Data.w Lifecycle}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

Reservoir flow used older ad hoc state names and shader-side release logic.
The Python analysis path and Vulkan path did not share the same simple
birth/live/death rule, and repeated reservoir columns could create artificial
starting collisions if unborn particles were included in contact processing.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

\texttt{SimpleReservoir} now uses \texttt{Particle.Data.w} as the lifecycle
field:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \(\texttt{Data.w} > 0\): unborn particle; value is the birth frame;
    \item \(\texttt{Data.w} = 0\): live particle;
    \item \(\texttt{Data.w} < 0\): dead particle.
\end{itemize}

Python \texttt{ForceDynamics} gates contact scans, wall scans, velocity
updates, position updates, and diagnostics by this live-state rule.  When a
particle reaches its birth frame, it is moved to the inlet birth position with
a \(1.1\) diameter offset and both position buffers are synchronized.  After
position update, a particle that crosses the outlet is marked dead.

The Vulkan reservoir compute path now calls
\texttt{StartReservoir(SourceID)} at the beginning of the source invocation and
\texttt{RetireParticlePastXMax(SourceID)} after \texttt{CalcPosition}.  This
matches the Python lifecycle rule while leaving \texttt{CalcPosition}
unchanged.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Particle solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The reservoir state is a source-local lifecycle gate, not a change to the
ForceDynamics force law.  Unborn and dead particles return before collision
processing, so repeated stored columns can exist in the input data without
generating artificial overlap, tunneling, wall contact, or diagnostic energy.
Live particles continue to use the ordinary source-owned overlap-area force
model.

The GLSL call order remains compatible with one source invocation:
\begin{verbatim}
if (!StartReservoir(SourceID))
    return;

// source-owned contact scans and force accumulation
CalcVelocity(SourceID, totalForce);
CalcPosition(SourceID);
RetireParticlePastXMax(SourceID);
\end{verbatim}

The current result is a particle solution because the reservoir lifecycle is
working in the active Python and Vulkan reservoir paths.  Broader throughput,
long-run statistical energy, and inlet packing studies remain future
validation work.

\subsection{SimpleReservoirPeriodic Vertex Lifecycle Output}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

\texttt{SimpleReservoirPeriodicP.vert} used the reservoir lifecycle gate to
return early for unborn and dead particles:

\begin{verbatim}
if (!ParticleLifeActive(index, uint(ShaderFlags.frameNum)))
    return;
\end{verbatim}

This prevented inactive particles from reaching the cell-occupancy insertion
code, but it also returned from the vertex shader before writing a defined
\texttt{gl\_Position}, point size, or output color.  In practice, inactive or
retired particles could still appear as stale visual artifacts, including a
red particle drawn near the center of the screen.  Increasing the cell-array
side length could hide other pressure symptoms, but it did not address the
undefined vertex output.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

Inactive reservoir particles now write a defined offscreen vertex before the
early return:

\begin{verbatim}
if (!ParticleLifeActive(index, uint(ShaderFlags.frameNum))) {
    gl_Position = vec4(2.0, 2.0, 2.0, 1.0);
    gl_PointSize = 0.0;
    fragColor = vec3(0.0);
    return;
}
\end{verbatim}

The offscreen clip-space position keeps the vertex outside the visible region,
while the zero point size and neutral color remove stale display state.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The fix does not change reservoir birth, death, collision detection, or
ForceDynamics.  It only makes the vertex-stage lifecycle gate produce defined
graphics output when a particle is not renderable or processable.  Because
\texttt{discard} is not available in vertex shaders, explicitly moving the
vertex outside clip space is the appropriate stage-local way to suppress
inactive particles.

This also clarifies the debugging interpretation: a red particle drawn near
the center was a vertex-output artifact, not proof that a dead particle was
being included in contact processing.  Compute-side lifecycle filtering still
uses \texttt{StartReservoir(SourceID)} for sources and skips targets whose
\texttt{Data.w} is not live.

\subsection{Velocity-Angle HSV Python Visualization}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The Python viewer used fixed particle colors with a collision-color override.
This made collision state easy to see, but it did not expose flow direction or
changes in particle velocity angle during reservoir and box tests.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

The Python viewer now supports \texttt{RUN\_CONFIGURATION.hsv\_color}.  When
enabled, \texttt{SimulationRunner.py} colors each active particle from
\texttt{VelRad.w} using \texttt{gbase/utilities.py:hsv\_angle}.  The helper
maps a radians angle to hue using \(1/(2\pi)\), clamps
\texttt{hsv\_sat} and \texttt{hsv\_val} to \([0,1]\), and returns a Python RGB
tuple.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The color change is diagnostic only.  It reads the velocity angle already
maintained by the dynamics model, but it does not feed color back into contact
detection, force accumulation, integration, reservoir lifecycle, or energy and
momentum reports.

Keeping the HSV helper in \texttt{gbase/utilities.py} and applying it in
\texttt{SimulationRunner.py} preserves the boundary between portable dynamics
logic and Python display logic.  A later GLSL utility can mirror the same
\texttt{hsv\_angle(angle, value, sat)} interface for Vulkan rendering without
changing the ForceDynamics collision model.

\subsection{BoundarySpheresAndCube Vulkan Visualization Parity}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

\texttt{BoundarySpheresAndCube} rendered particles with fixed collision
colors and a sphere mesh whose size was baked from the scenario radius during
vertex-buffer creation.  The generated ForceDynamics GLSL force path applied
contacts, but it bypassed the Python diagnostic contact-slot code that set
\texttt{colFlg}, so shader collision colors could fail to reflect current
contacts.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

The rendered sphere resource now stores a unit-radius mesh, and the
\texttt{BoundarySpheresAndCube} sphere vertex shader scales each instance by
the particle radius in \texttt{P[index].Data.x}.  The compute shader clears
\texttt{P[SourceID].colFlg} once per source invocation, and
\texttt{AccumulateContactForce(...)} sets it when a valid particle or wall
contact is accumulated.

\texttt{BoundarySpheresAndCube} also supports scenario-controlled HSV
velocity-angle rendering through \texttt{HSV\_ON}, \texttt{HSV\_SAT}, and
\texttt{HSV\_VAL} in \texttt{params.glsl}.  The shared GLSL utility
\texttt{colorizeVelocity(angle, sat, val)} reads \texttt{VelRad.w} in radians
and maps it to hue with \(1/(2\pi)\).

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The result separates display diagnostics from dynamics.  Particle radius,
velocity angle, and collision state are read from the particle ABI, while the
renderer owns mesh scaling and color conversion.  Collision coloring and HSV
coloring are now selectable visual interpretations of the same dynamics state.
Neither feeds back into contact detection, force accumulation, integration, or
energy and momentum diagnostics.

Because \texttt{ForceDynamics.glsl} is generated content, the
\texttt{colFlg} assignment in \texttt{AccumulateContactForce(...)} should be
moved into the exporter template when the generated file is regenerated.

\subsection{Crowding Requires Internal Momentum, Not Energy Repair}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The reservoir boundary-particle wall test converted solid pipe walls from
manual configured planes into cell-occupancy boundary markers.  A mobile
particle that encounters a \texttt{ptype=1.0} boundary marker evaluates a wall
segment and applies the ordinary source-owned wall force.

Under crowding, particles near the wall can compress into the particles behind
them.  The observed failure mode is tunneling in the crowded particle column,
not a failure of the boundary marker itself.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

The project rule is now momentum-first: energy is diagnostic bookkeeping, not
a governing correction.  No global step should reach backward to force a
kinetic-energy or total-energy value.  A particle responds only to the current
geometry, current force, current momentum, and stored local contact state.

When overlap compression cannot be resolved as translational motion during the
current frame, the unresolved part should be treated as internal normal
momentum or contact stress.  The stored quantity is local and causal.  It must
later release only through current contact geometry and current contact
normals.  This is the pressure channel required for crowded contacts.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Analysis direction.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The crowding case exposes a missing physical channel.  In a real material,
pressure information propagates through contacts at a finite wave speed,
related to the material speed of sound.  The entire particle column does not
know the future rebound path; pressure builds and propagates contact by
contact.

Therefore the next model step should not be a maximum-penetration clamp or an
energy correction.  A clamp can be retained as a diagnostic failure threshold,
but it does not explain where the impulse went.  The correct bookkeeping
question is whether the current impulse became translational particle
momentum, wall/constraint momentum, or stored internal contact momentum.

For a particle-particle crowding contact, the internal state should be scalar
normal momentum owned by the contact or contact pair.  Direction must come
from the current contact normal at release time, not from a stored arbitrary
vector.  For a wall contact, the same idea represents local pressure against
the wall segment or constraint.  Energy diagnostics may later report the
consequence of this bookkeeping, but they must not govern the update.

\subsection{GLSL Wall Models And Boundary-Particle Export}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The generated \texttt{ForceDynamics.glsl} path was validated for ordinary
particle contacts and manual wall scans, but the scenario compute wrappers
owned different hardcoded wall loops.  Simple walls scanned flags \(1..4\),
pipe/reservoir walls scanned flags \(3..4\), and boundary-particle walls were
still a Python-side model rule.  Regenerating the common include could also
drop hand-maintained reservoir helpers such as \texttt{StartReservoir} and
\texttt{RetireParticlePastXMax}.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

\texttt{ForceDynamics.glsl} now exports reusable boundary-particle wall
helpers:

\begin{verbatim}
IsBoundaryParticle
BoundaryParticleWallFlag
EvaluateWallSegment
ProcessBoundaryParticleWallCollision
\end{verbatim}

The scenario \texttt{*P.comp} wrapper selects exactly one wall model:

\begin{verbatim}
WALL_MODEL_SIMPLE
WALL_MODEL_PIPE
WALL_MODEL_BOUNDARY_PARTICLE
\end{verbatim}

Simple wrappers scan wall flags \(1..4\).  Pipe wrappers scan wall flags
\(3..4\).  Boundary-particle wrappers do not run a manual wall scan; they
route \texttt{ptype=1.0} target records from cell occupancy to
\texttt{ProcessBoundaryParticleWallCollision}.  The exporter also emits
\texttt{StartReservoir} and \texttt{RetireParticlePastXMax} as custom GLSL
helpers so reservoir wrappers survive regeneration of the common include.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution for wall-model wrapper selection and boundary-particle export.
Tunneling detection remains an error-detection extension.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The important architectural separation is now explicit.  The generated
dynamics include owns reusable force and geometry functions.  The stable
scenario wrapper owns contact discovery, lifecycle gates, and wall-model
selection.  Boundary-particle locality is supplied by the GPU cell occupancy
structure; the Python-only \texttt{BoundaryParticleAppliesToSource} helper is
not exported because it only imitates that locality during direct scans.

The current contact queries remain sampled overlap tests.  They can report
contact when the current distance is inside the radius tolerance, but they do
not yet perform swept crossing tests.  Tunneling should therefore be detected
as an error in the hand-maintained contact-query or wall-segment shader path,
not hidden as a dynamics correction.

\subsection{Vertical Boundary-Particle Reservoir Flow In Vulkan}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The boundary-particle reservoir Vulkan path initially reused the horizontal
reservoir assumptions.  Boundary marker contacts could be routed through the
boundary-particle wall path, but the reservoir lifecycle helpers still placed
newly born particles and retired escaped particles along the \(x\) direction.
The active wall-segment function and the reservoir flow direction were also
easy to confuse because both appeared in generated shader configuration.

\subsubsection{<current form at that stage>}

The generated boundary configuration now separates the two selectors:

\begin{verbatim}
#define WALL_FUNC vertical_wall
#define PERIODIC_DIRECTION vertical
\end{verbatim}

At that stage, \texttt{WALL\_FUNC} chose the boundary-marker wall-segment evaluator:
\texttt{EvaluateHorizontalWallSegment} or
\texttt{EvaluateVerticalWallSegment}.  \texttt{PERIODIC\_DIRECTION} chooses
the reservoir lifecycle direction.  When it is \texttt{vertical},
\texttt{StartReservoir(SourceID)} preserves the generated \(x\) coordinate
and moves the particle to \(y_{\min}+2.2r\).  The existing
\texttt{RetireParticlePastXMax(SourceID)} wrapper-compatible helper then
retires the particle when its next position passes \(y_{\max}\).  When the
selector is not vertical, the historical \(x\)-direction behavior remains.

The later Python self-describing-marker migration supersedes the
\texttt{WALL\_FUNC} evaluator decision: marker \texttt{Data.z} now selects the
wall evaluator.  \texttt{PERIODIC\_DIRECTION} remains a separate lifecycle
direction.  Vulkan synchronization with marker-owned evaluator IDs is the next
porting step.

During validation, apparent downward screen motion was traced to the viewing
transform rather than to the reservoir force or lifecycle rule.  In
\texttt{Resource::GeneralViewing}, the configured zero-angle view still
receives hard-coded \(180^\circ\) rotations about \(z\) and \(y\), and the
Vulkan projection applies the normal clip-space y correction.  Therefore a
positive simulation \(y\) velocity can appear as downward motion depending on
the current view.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution for generated GLSL wall-function selection and vertical
reservoir lifecycle selection.  View-orientation cleanup remains a display
task.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The key correction was not to infer reservoir flow from wall geometry inside
the dynamics law.  Wall response and lifecycle direction are separate
configuration facts:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{WALL\_FUNC} describes the wall segment represented by a
    boundary marker.
    \item \texttt{PERIODIC\_DIRECTION} describes the axis used for reservoir
    birth and retirement.
\end{itemize}

This keeps the generated \texttt{ForceDynamics.glsl} reusable while letting
the scenario wrapper and generated config includes own scenario-specific
selection.  The remaining view issue should be diagnosed as rendering
orientation, not particle dynamics, unless direct position/velocity reports
show negative \(y\) motion in simulation coordinates.

\subsection{Depth Default For Contact Force Measure}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

\texttt{ForceDynamics} originally used overlap area directly as the contact
force measure:
\begin{equation}
F_{ij}=q_{ij}A_{ij}.
\end{equation}
This made overlap-area geometry both the diagnostic quantity and the default
force magnitude input.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

\texttt{ForceDynamics} now keeps overlap area in Python diagnostics and keeps
area mode available, but the default configuration uses penetration depth:
\begin{verbatim}
contact_force_measure = "depth";
\end{verbatim}
The generated GLSL path receives the same selection through:
\begin{verbatim}
#define CONTACT_FORCE_MEASURE depth
\end{verbatim}
In depth mode the force measure is \(r_i+r_j-d_{ij}\) for particle contacts
and the analogous wall-ghost penetration depth for wall contacts.  In area
mode the earlier \(A_{ij}\) measure is used.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

The default force law is simpler and cheaper to reason about while preserving
overlap-area reporting and comparison.  Existing stiffness values now act as
depth stiffness values, so the project reset the Python generation cfg
\texttt{collision\_stiffness\_q} values to a smaller baseline.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

Area remains useful as a conservative geometric diagnostic, but penetration
depth is the more direct instantaneous compression measure for the active
force calculation.  The selectable mode keeps the older area model available
without forcing diagnostics, plots, or historical reports to change names in
the same step.

\subsection{Boundary-Particle Reservoir Cell-Guard Margin}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The boundary-particle reservoir generator derived the cell-array side length
from the number of particles per release row and the configured particles per
cell row.  The physical pipe walls were then placed very near the cell-array
minimum and maximum bounds.  This worked with \texttt{wall\_guard}, because
particles were retired only by the selected wall/outlet rule, but it created a
failure mode for \texttt{cell\_guard}.

With \texttt{cell\_guard}, a particle approaching a boundary-particle pipe
wall could also enter the cell-array guard band.  The left/min side was
protected by the inlet exemption for vertical reservoir flow, but the right/max
side still used the general upper cell guard:
\begin{verbatim}
next_position.x >= float(WIDTH - 2u)
\end{verbatim}
for vertical flow.  Therefore particles near the right pipe wall could be
retired as escaped particles even though they were still inside the intended
physical pipe.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

The generator has been changed so boundary-particle reservoirs reserve an
internal guard margin between the physical pipe walls and the cell-array
guard.  For vertical boundary-particle flow, the physical pipe walls are moved
inward on \(x\); for horizontal boundary-particle flow, they are moved inward
on \(y\).  Mobile release positions are shifted by the same margin so new
particles are born inside the physical pipe, not inside the cell guard band.

During Vulkan validation, the generated shader files still showed the earlier
cell-array size and boundary values:
\begin{verbatim}
WIDTH = 27
BOUNDARY_XMAX = 26.00
\end{verbatim}
With these values, the max-side cell guard remains close to the old pipe wall.
The immediate next step is therefore to stop relying on a fully inferred side
length and manually set the cell-array side length for this reservoir family.
The generator should use that configured side length to place the physical
pipe walls with an explicit guard margin inside the cell-array bounds.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Partial solution.  The reason for the max-side retirement was identified, and
the generator now has the first margin-aware geometry rule.  The next
implementation step is to make cell-array side length manually selectable so
the physical pipe, guard band, and generated Vulkan constants agree.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The important design distinction is that boundary particles define physical
wall response, while \texttt{cell\_guard} defines cell-array safety.  These are
not the same boundary.  When the physical wall is placed too close to the
cell-array guard, the guard becomes an accidental wall-death rule and prevents
the boundary-particle wall model from doing its work.

For boundary-particle reservoirs, the generated layout must satisfy:
\begin{verbatim}
cell-array min/max
    guard margin
        physical boundary-particle wall

\subsection{CD Nozzle Reservoir Generator}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The project had straight boundary-particle reservoir generators for horizontal
and vertical pipe-wall tests.  The older MATLAB CD nozzle scripts generated a
boundary-particle nozzle by sweeping a piecewise radius profile along an axial
direction, but that geometry was not yet represented as a Python generator.
The MATLAB setup also encoded fixed section lengths directly in
\texttt{GetDevalleRadius.m}, so entry length, convergence length, throat
length, divergence length, and exit length were not separately configurable.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

\texttt{BoundaryCDNozzleReservoir.py} began as a boundary-only generator.  In
that first form, it did not generate mobile reservoir particles and did not
run birth/death lifecycle setup.  The generated particle records were boundary
markers:
\begin{verbatim}
ptype = 1.0
Data.w = 0.0
\end{verbatim}
and \texttt{dispatchx} remained one because there were no mobile source
particles to process.

The nozzle profile is parameterized by section lengths and radii:
\begin{verbatim}
dim
nozzle_inlet_length
nozzle_converge_length
nozzle_throat_length
nozzle_diverge_length
nozzle_exit_length
nozzle_inlet_radius
nozzle_throat_radius
nozzle_exit_radius
\end{verbatim}
The total axial length is calculated from the section lengths:
\[
L =
L_{\mathrm{inlet}}+
L_{\mathrm{converge}}+
L_{\mathrm{throat}}+
L_{\mathrm{diverge}}+
L_{\mathrm{exit}}.
\]

When \texttt{dim=2}, the generator writes the upper and lower nozzle profile
in a single \(z\) plane.  The 2D profile plane now defaults to \(z=2.0\), so
the nozzle lies in the same shallow layer used by the other two-dimensional
particle studies.  When \texttt{dim=3}, the generator writes circular boundary
marker rings along the axial direction.

The generator now also creates mobile reservoir particles.  Mobile particles
are written first with \texttt{ptype=0.0}; boundary marker particles are
written after the active particles with \texttt{ptype=1.0}.  Therefore
\texttt{dispatchx} is written as the number of active particles plus the null

particles.  The mobile spacing is:
\[
2r(1+\texttt{fraction\_of\_diameter\_separation})
\]
and the frame spacing is calculated from that distance, the timestep, and the
configured forward reservoir speed.

The nozzle entrance is anchored by a start-plane convention.  For horizontal
flow, \texttt{nozzle\_start\_x} defaults to \(1.0\); for vertical flow,
\texttt{nozzle\_start\_y} defaults to \(1.0\).  The process inlet/outlet
values written as \texttt{wallXMIN}/\texttt{wallXMAX} or
\texttt{wallYMIN}/\texttt{wallYMAX} now describe the reservoir lifecycle
planes, not the radius envelope around the nozzle centerline.  This prevents
Vulkan \texttt{StartReservoir} from moving born particles to the middle of the
cell array.

For visual validation, the generator writes:
\begin{verbatim}
vulkan/sim/BoundaryCDNozzleReservoir/CDNozzleGenerated.obj
\end{verbatim}
The OBJ is a triangle mesh generated from the same radius profile as the

boundary particle markers and \texttt{EvaluateWallSegment}.  The OBJ emits
\texttt{v}, \texttt{vt}, and \texttt{vn} records and faces in
\texttt{v/vt/vn} form so it is compatible with the current simple Vulkan OBJ
loader.

The CD nozzle wall response is now represented by:
\begin{verbatim}
boundary_particle_function = "cd_nozzle_wall";
\end{verbatim}

\texttt{EvaluateCDNozzleWallSegment}.  Boundary markers provide broad-phase
locality and upper/lower wall identity, while the evaluator calculates the
actual wall point and wall normal from the analytic CD nozzle profile at the
source particle's current axial coordinate.  The Python direct-scan loop now
lets the selected evaluator decide whether a marker produces a valid segment,
instead of using the old flat \texttt{BoundaryParticleWallFlag} pre-filter.

The GLSL exporter now emits the corresponding CD nozzle functions:
\begin{verbatim}
BoundaryParticleCDNozzleWallFlag
CDNozzleRadius
CDNozzleRadiusSlope
EvaluateCDNozzleWallSegment
\end{verbatim}
and the generated \texttt{EvaluateWallSegment} dispatcher includes the

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Particle solution for geometry generation, reservoir staging, Python curved
wall response, and generated GLSL CD nozzle wall-function export.  Vulkan
runtime validation of the generated CD nozzle wall response remains the next
test step.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The CD nozzle should not be treated as another horizontal or vertical pipe
case.  It is a boundary-particle geometry family with a radius function.  The
generator's first responsibility is to produce a trustworthy marker cloud; the
dynamics law should then receive those markers through the same
boundary-particle wall path used by straight walls.

The generated OBJ exposed one Vulkan rendering distinction: OBJ boundaries
are loaded as non-indexed vertex lists, while the old cube boundary uses an
indexed cube draw.  The command buffer now selects the draw method from
\texttt{boundary\_as\_obj}: OBJ boundaries use \texttt{vkCmdDraw}; cube
boundaries retain \texttt{vkCmdDrawIndexed}.  This preserves old cube and
sphere rendering while allowing generated nozzle OBJ visualization.

The current curve-aware wall function is selected by configuration:
\begin{verbatim}
boundary_particle_function = "cd_nozzle_wall";
\end{verbatim}
In 2D this function evaluates the local nozzle profile curve and returns the
wall normal, penetration/overlap geometry, wall flag, and validity.  In 3D the
corresponding radial wall evaluator from the nozzle centerline remains future
work.  The source-owned force accumulation rule does not change; only the
wall-segment evaluator changes.

\subsection{Reservoir Birth Admission Must Reject Starting Overlap}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

Reservoir particles carry a scheduled frame in \texttt{Data.w}.  When the
frame arrives, Python and GLSL immediately place the particle at the inlet,
set \texttt{Data.w=0}, and process it as live.  If an earlier particle has
been pushed back into the inlet region, the new particle can therefore be born
inside that live particle.  Ordinary collision processing sees the overlap
only after admission and may report a starting collision or tunneling error.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

The approved next rule separates a birth attempt from successful admission.
\texttt{Data.w} remains the lifecycle authority, while reserved
\texttt{colFlg} values describe the attempt:

\begin{verbatim}
colFlg = 2  waiting
colFlg = 3  wants to become live
colFlg = 0  admitted live
\end{verbatim}

At its scheduled frame, a candidate is tested only against frame-start live
mobile targets.  Boundary markers, dead particles, unborn particles, and other
birth candidates do not block the test.  The contact query must return a
distinct \texttt{STARTING\_COLLISION} result so the wrapper can terminate the
remaining neighbor search.  A blocked candidate returns to \texttt{colFlg=2},
sets \texttt{Data.w=frameNum+1}, and receives no force or motion update.  An
unblocked candidate sets \texttt{Data.w=0} and \texttt{colFlg=0} before
entering ordinary dynamics.

Each blocked particle is delayed independently.  Later particles retain their
original scheduled frames, preserving the generated release schedule rather
than shifting an entire reservoir wave train.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

No solution yet.  The lifecycle and contact-return contract is documented, but
the current Python and GLSL paths still activate particles immediately.  The
next work is to implement and test deferred birth admission.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

A boolean contact result can report overlap but cannot tell the wrapper whether
it found an ordinary live collision or a birth-blocking starting collision.
The distinct result is needed because the latter must end the source search and
skip all force and integration work for that frame.

The GPU implementation also has an ordering constraint.  If one candidate
writes itself live while another candidate is still scanning targets, results
can depend on invocation scheduling.  Admission must therefore read immutable
frame-start lifecycle state and commit successful births in a separate phase or
through an equivalent synchronized design.  This preserves source ownership:
each invocation decides and writes only its own lifecycle state.

\subsection{Boundary Marker Configuration Becomes Self-Checking}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

Boundary wall evaluation also depended on a scenario-wide wall-function
selection.  A configuration could place its retirement plane on or before the

Calculated geometry should be authoritative once generation begins, but manual

\subsection{Adjacent Markers Can Represent One Physical Wall}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

Boundary-marker duplicate suppression used marker \texttt{TargetID}.  This
prevents processing one marker repeatedly when several source corners discover
the same occupancy record, but it does not recognize two different markers as
representations of one physical wall.

At a rounded CD-nozzle profile step, the ending marker of one horizontal row
and the starting marker of the adjacent row can occupy neighboring cells.  A
mobile particle whose extent spans both cells can discover both target IDs.
Both markers then evaluate the same analytic upper or lower wall at the source
axial coordinate, so target-ID-only suppression can accumulate the same wall
force twice.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

Python evaluates the candidate segment and records its physical wall flag in
\texttt{processed\_boundary\_walls}.  The periodic and CD Vulkan wrappers now
use the equivalent source-local wall bit mask.  Only the first valid segment
for each wall flag contributes force during a frame; marker target IDs are not
inserted into the ordinary particle duplicate list.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full solution for the current Python, periodic Vulkan, and CD Vulkan wrappers.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

Marker identity belongs to the broad phase, while wall flag identifies the
physical response.  The GLSL wrappers maintain a source-local processed-wall
mask keyed by \texttt{BoundaryWallSegment.wallFlag}.  They evaluate a candidate
first, accumulate only the first valid segment for that flag, and then set the
flag bit.  Ordinary mobile-particle duplicate suppression remains target-ID
based.

\subsection{Capture Diagnostics Expose Artificial Inlet Compression}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

The Vulkan particle structure could be captured, but reservoir behavior was
judged mainly from rendered motion.  Scheduled waves were admitted when their
stored frame arrived.  Release spacing assumed that the preceding wave
continued at its configured inlet speed and cleared the birth position.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

The Python capture reader now pairs an original particle binary with adjacent
\texttt{<name>\_<frame>.cap} files.  It writes \texttt{<name>.csv} beside the
original binary and reports lifecycle counts, momentum, kinetic energy,
same-cohort initial baselines, and reverse-flow momentum and kinetic energy.
Reverse flow is selected by a negative velocity projection onto the initial
flow direction.

Frame-by-frame captures isolated one inlet lane.  At frame 5722 an older
particle remained at $x=1.16888058$ when a new particle was admitted at
$x=1.22000003$.  For radius $0.1$, their center spacing was only
$0.05111945$, producing artificial starting penetration of $0.14888055$.
Across frames 5722--5725, pair momentum remained approximately $0.02449093$,
while pair kinetic energy rose from approximately $0.00021008$ to
$0.00118541$.  The older particle reversed and the new particle accelerated
downstream, producing the observed counterflow line.

The selected next model removes scheduled runtime birth.  A finite reservoir
will be prefilled with nonoverlapping particles.  At release, the particles
and rear wall begin moving together; the rear wall continues at inlet velocity
as a piston.  The long reservoir will use a rectangular cell domain to avoid
the memory cost of a cubic array.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

Full diagnostic explanation of the counterflow source.  The staged-birth
model is not repaired; it is superseded as the next development direction by
the prefilled moving-piston reservoir.

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

Scheduled frame separation is only a prediction based on unconstrained inlet
speed.  It cannot guarantee clearance once downstream contacts slow or return
a particle to the inlet.  Materializing a new particle inside that live
particle creates compression without a causal mechanical source.  Momentum
can remain pair-consistent while kinetic energy grows because the initial
overlap supplies artificial contact potential.  A prefilled reservoir removes
that discontinuity.  Its piston is an explicit external momentum source, so
future diagnostics must include piston impulse rather than treating particle
momentum as a closed-system quantity.

\subsection{Starting-Contact Effective-Radius Tunneling Defect}

\subsubsection{<previous form>}

Ordinary particle contact originally used the physical source and target
radii directly.  Contact existed whenever the physical center distance
satisfied
\begin{equation}
d_{ij}<r_i+r_j,
\end{equation}
and the physical penetration depth was
\begin{equation}
\delta_{ij}=r_i+r_j-d_{ij}.
\end{equation}
There was no interval in which physically overlapping particles were hidden
from contact force or tunneling detection.

Commit \texttt{20b4881}, dated June 18, 2026 at 11:23 AM, introduced special
starting-contact geometry.  Its commit title was \emph{update git ignore}, but
the commit also added approximately 300 lines to
\texttt{ForceDynamicsBase.py}.  The added model scaled both radii by
\begin{equation}
s=\frac{d_{ij}(0)}{r_i+r_j}
\end{equation}
and used
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{eff}}=s r_i,
\qquad
r_j^{\mathrm{eff}}=s r_j
\end{equation}
so an initially overlapping pair appeared to have zero effective
penetration.  Commit \texttt{c41ded4}, later on June 18, moved this logic into
\texttt{ForceDynamics.py}; it did not originate the model.

The intent was to avoid an immediate force discontinuity when a configured
initial state already contained overlap.  The contact could compress relative
to its initial separation and rebound to that separation.  Once it returned,
the state became \texttt{resolved}.  Force geometry was then suppressed until
the physical center distance reached the full physical separation
$(r_i+r_j)$.

\subsubsection{<current form>}

\texttt{TwoParticleHorizontalBug001.cfg} provides a narrow Python
reproduction.  Two radius-(0.25) particles begin near the right wall with a
small vertical offset.  The second particle is compressed between the first
particle and the wall.  After the initial pair response and later wall
rebounds, the pair remains physically overlapped while its starting-contact
state is resolved and suppressed.

The frame reports capture every particle and momentum frame from 0 through
400.  The first reported pair phase ends at frame 25 when the effective
overlap returns to zero.  The physical particles do not separate to their
diameter of (0.5); their center distance remains near (0.42) for much of
the subsequent motion.  Because the state remains suppressed, the later
approach toward the left wall produces no valid force geometry.

The center ordering reverses between frames 381 and 382:
\begin{align}
x_2-x_1 &= 0.01206345 && \text{at frame 381},\\
x_2-x_1 &=-0.00229142 && \text{at frame 382}.
\end{align}
The particles therefore pass through one another while still listed as a
physically overlapping pair.  Before the reporting correction,
\texttt{contacts.csv} represented these suppressed detections as active
contact rows whose normal, distance, depth, and force were all zero.  It also
wrote empty rows for every boundary marker, obscuring the two mobile-particle
history.

Python tunneling diagnostics now test the physical proximity
\begin{equation}
p_{ij}^{\mathrm{phys}}=d_{ij}-r_j
\end{equation}
before effective-radius or starting-contact logic.  A negative physical
proximity reports \texttt{ERROR\_PARTICLE\_TUNNELING} with source and target
IDs.  This diagnostic change does not alter force, velocity, position, or
internal momentum.

Reporting now distinguishes valid force contacts from physical detections
whose geometry is suppressed.  Valid rows use
\texttt{contact\_status=active} and \texttt{geometry\_valid=1}; suppressed
rows use \texttt{contact\_status=suppressed} and
\texttt{geometry\_valid=0}.  Null, dead, and boundary-marker particles are
excluded from mobile contact reports.  Momentum reports include the runtime
error code, description, source, target, and wall.  Every new simulation run
clears all files directly inside its configured report directory before
writing new output.

\subsubsection{difference outcome}

particle solution

\subsubsection{<Analysis>}

The defect is not primarily a tolerance, force-stiffness, or timestep problem.
It is a violation of the contact-geometry invariant.  The effective-radius
model creates the interval
\begin{equation}
d_{ij}(0)<d_{ij}<r_i+r_j
\end{equation}
in which the real particles overlap but the force model treats the contact as
open.  If relative motion reverses before complete physical separation, the
particles can recompress and exchange ordering without force, maximum-depth
containment, or the former effective-radius tunneling check.  This is a
history-dependent transparent-overlap region.

The model replacement must preserve physical radii for all geometry.  Physical
overlap must always create physical contact, and contact may end only at
$(d_{ij}\ge r_i+r_j)$.  Starting overlap must instead initialize local
compression state and its corresponding potential-energy baseline.  Contact
history may control stored momentum, phase, and constitutive response, but it
must never resize particles or suppress physically valid geometry.  The same
rule applies to starting wall penetration.

The reporting and physical tunneling checks provide a verified diagnostic
surface, but the dynamics correction remains intentionally pending.  The next
implementation step is to remove effective-radius scaling and resolved-contact
suppression, initialize starting compression from physical penetration, and
rerun \texttt{TwoParticleHorizontalBug001} as the regression case.

\subsection{Function-Wall Boundary Model Direction}

\subsubsection{previous form}

The generic wall path moved toward fully parametric curve segments using
\texttt{x(t)} and \texttt{y(t)}.  Runtime contact used a bounded closest-point
search: sample candidate \texttt{t} values, choose the nearest sample, refine
with Newton iteration, clamp \texttt{t} to \([0,1]\), and then calculate the
wall tangent and normal at that closest point.

That representation is powerful, but it is broader than the current model
needs.  The active two-dimensional conduit walls are intended to be smooth,
single-valued functions of position, not looping or self-overlapping curves.
The boundary particle is also not meant to be the wall geometry itself.  Its
job is to mark that a cell contains a wall so the mobile particle can be sent
to the wall evaluator.  The evaluator should then use the mobile particle's
\(x,y\) position and the wall definition to calculate the physical response.

The leakage seen near the start of the converging section exposed this
confusion.  Small particles could enter cells near the wall transition without
getting a reliable wall response.  Treating marker centers as geometry, or
making marker rasterization increasingly dense, does not solve the underlying
model issue.  It only changes which cells happen to trigger the evaluator.

\subsubsection{current form}

The next boundary model should use function walls for the active Python and
simple GLSL work.  A wall segment is evaluated directly from the mobile
particle position:
\[
    y_{wall} = f(x_{particle}).
\]
For cubic function segments this can be represented as
\[
    y = y_0 + a_1 dx + a_2 dx^2 + a_3 dx^3,
    \qquad dx = x - x_0.
\]
The local slope is
\[
    \frac{dy}{dx} = a_1 + 2a_2 dx + 3a_3 dx^2.
\]
The wall definition, or the generator, must provide the outward normal
orientation for the segment.  Runtime code should not need separate lower-wall
and upper-wall branches.  Once the outward normal is known, every wall uses the
same signed-distance calculation:
\[
    d_{out} = (p - p_{wall}) \cdot n_{out},
\]
where \(p\) is the particle center, \(p_{wall}\) is the point on the wall at
that particle position, and \(n_{out}\) is the outward wall normal.  The
penetration depth is then
\[
    \delta = r + d_{out}.
\]
A contact exists when \(\delta > 0\).  The response force acts inward, opposite
the outward normal.

Vertical walls are the only required special case.  They can be represented as
\(x = c\) with a configured outward normal.  They still use the same signed
normal penetration formula after selecting the wall point
\((c, y_{particle})\).

Boundary markers remain broad-phase sentinels.  A marker says that the cell is
near a wall; it does not define the wall's position, tangent, normal, or
penetration depth.  The physical geometry comes from the function-wall segment
and its normal orientation.

\subsection{Compressor Piston Bring-Up}

\subsubsection{previous form}

The reservoir piston was present in generated metadata, but Python runtime
configuration did not consistently see the same piston model.  Source cfg
files provided \texttt{piston\_velocity} and
\texttt{piston\_start\_frame}; generated test files provided
\texttt{piston\_velocity\_x/y/z}, \texttt{piston\_x\_start}, and
\texttt{piston\_x\_stop}.  The runtime dynamics required
\texttt{piston\_enabled}, \texttt{piston\_velocity},
\texttt{piston\_x\_start}, \texttt{piston\_x\_stop}, and
\texttt{piston\_start\_frame}.  Running directly from the source cfg could
therefore report the piston as disabled or place it at zero with zero
velocity.

The first piston contact geometry also used an offset ghost-particle
construction.  That made the visual piston line and the physical contact
plane disagree.  A particle could visually reach the piston before the
dynamics treated the piston plane as physically present.

\subsubsection{current form}

Runtime configuration now normalizes both source cfg and generated test-file
forms.  When scalar generated fields are present,
\texttt{piston\_velocity\_x/y/z} are converted into the vector
\texttt{piston\_velocity}.  When a source cfg provides
\texttt{piston\_velocity} but omits \texttt{piston\_enabled}, the piston is
enabled.  When source cfg files omit derived piston bounds, the runtime derives
\texttt{piston\_x\_start} and \texttt{piston\_x\_stop} from the reservoir
boundary curves and \texttt{piston\_start\_offset}, matching the generator.

The Python piston contact is now an analytic plane contact at the displayed
piston position.  Penetration is calculated from the particle left edge and
the piston plane:
\[
    \delta = r - (x_{particle}-x_{piston}).
\]
A contact exists when \(\delta>0\).  This makes the white piston line,
contact geometry, and moving-wall velocity refer to the same physical plane.

The Python viewer now includes piston diagnostics.  With dynamics diagnostics
enabled, the UI prints piston position and velocity near the first mobile
particle.  The viewer also supports a particle-diameter grid so rendered
radius, world scale, and dynamics radius can be checked visually.

compression limit.

at failure: target IDs, wall flags, penetration depths, target depth, hard
depth, candidate velocity, contained velocity, and blocked momentum.  That
