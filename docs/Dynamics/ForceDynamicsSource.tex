\section{ForceDynamics Source Flow}

\newcommand{\ForceFunctionSection}[1]{%
  \subsection{ForceDynamics:#1}%
  \label{force:#1}%
}

\texttt{ForceDynamics} treats contact geometry as an instantaneous central
repulsive force field.  Python still calculates and records overlap area for
diagnostics and for the optional area-force mode.  The current default
configuration uses penetration depth as the force measure:
\begin{verbatim}
contact_force_measure = "depth";
\end{verbatim}
The alternate supported mode is:
\begin{verbatim}
contact_force_measure = "area";
\end{verbatim}
One source-particle invocation detects all current particle and wall contacts,
immediately vector-sums their forces into a source-local \texttt{totalForce},
and writes only the source velocity and position.  The implementation is
shaped to transfer directly to one GLSL compute invocation per source
particle.

\texttt{ForceDynamics} currently supports three wall interpretations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{simple walls}: all four configured wall planes are solid;
    \item \emph{pipe walls}: the configured \(y_{\min}\) and \(y_{\max}\)
    planes are solid while \(x_{\min}\) and \(x_{\max}\) are lifecycle inlet
    and outlet planes;
    \item \emph{boundary-particle walls}: particles with \texttt{ptype=1.0}
    are occupancy markers that represent local wall segments.
\end{itemize}

Simple and pipe walls use the configured wall-ghost geometry.  Boundary
particles are not mobile dynamics sources.  They remain active for display
and broad-phase placement, but when a mobile source sees a boundary marker as
a target, it evaluates the represented wall segment and accumulates a wall
force instead of a particle-particle force.

The current generated GLSL path validates the ordinary particle and wall force
model in Vulkan.  Starting-contact effective geometry remains Python-side
model behavior until the \texttt{StartingContactState.glsl} buffer/export path
is implemented, so \texttt{ResolveStartingCollision} is intentionally excluded
from the current Vulkan export validation.

\ForceFunctionSection{CollisionRun}

\texttt{CollisionRun} is the Python harness for the shader-shaped execution
schedule:

\begin{verbatim}
snapshot frame-start positions and velocities
initialize starting-contact effective geometry
initialize totalForce for every source
for each SourceID:
    scan possible targets
        if TargetID is a mobile particle:
            ProcessParticleCollision(TargetID, SourceID, totalForce)
        if TargetID is a boundary marker:
            ProcessBoundaryParticleWallCollision(SourceID, TargetID, totalForce)
for each SourceID:
    if boundary-particle walls are not enabled:
    for each configured wall flag selected by the wall mode:
        ProcessWallCollision(SourceID, wall, totalForce)
capture frame-start pair diagnostics
for each SourceID:
    CalcVelocity(SourceID, totalForce)
for each SourceID:
    CalcPosition(SourceID)
snapshot end positions for diagnostics
\end{verbatim}

The Python harness uses a direct particle scan where GLSL uses cell occupancy
lists.  Both schedules complete one source-local force sum before integration.
Starting-contact state is initialized after the frame-start snapshot so
particle-particle and particle-wall overlaps present in the initial
configuration can be treated as zero-force contact limits.  The end-position
snapshot is diagnostic only and is not a second force scan.

The Python harness uses a direct scan over particles where GLSL uses cell
occupancy lists.  Boundary-particle wall mode changes the meaning of a target
record during that scan; it does not change source ownership or introduce a
target write.

\ForceFunctionSection{InitializeStartingContactState}

\texttt{InitializeStartingContactState()} records mobile particle and manual
wall contacts that already overlap when the model starts.  Boundary marker
particles are not recorded as mobile starting contacts.  For each starting
contact, the current center distance becomes the effective separation limit.
The physical radii are still retained, but force and potential energy use the
effective geometry until the contact rebounds to its starting separation and
then clears the physical overlap.

\ForceFunctionSection{ProcessParticleCollision}

\texttt{ProcessParticleCollision(TargetID, SourceID, totalForce)} initializes
the current contact geometry, records a diagnostic contact slot, and
immediately adds the contact force to the source-local \texttt{totalForce}.
It does not write the target particle.  Boundary markers are not processed by
this function; they are routed to the boundary-particle wall path.

\ForceFunctionSection{ProcessWallCollision}

\texttt{ProcessWallCollision(SourceID, wall, totalForce)} creates the
configured wall ghost geometry and immediately adds its force to
\texttt{totalForce}.  In simple-wall mode the selected wall flags are
\(1,2,3,4\).  In pipe-wall mode the selected wall flags are \(3,4\), because
the \(x\) planes are lifecycle inlet/outlet planes rather than solid walls.
When boundary-particle walls are enabled, the manual wall scan is suppressed.

\ForceFunctionSection{ProcessBoundaryParticleWallCollision}

\texttt{ProcessBoundaryParticleWallCollision(SourceID, BoundaryID,
totalForce)} treats a boundary marker target as a source-owned wall contact.
It evaluates the wall segment represented by the boundary marker, appends the
ordinary wall diagnostic contact slot, and immediately accumulates that wall
force into \texttt{totalForce}.

\ForceFunctionSection{EvaluateWallSegment}

\texttt{EvaluateWallSegment(SourceID, BoundaryID)} is the boundary-particle
wall geometry dispatcher.  In Python, the target boundary marker owns its
evaluator ID in \texttt{Data.z}; the dispatcher does not read a global wall-
function selection.  Evaluator ID zero or an unknown ID produces no wall
segment, so older binary files must be regenerated.  The supported evaluators
are:

\begin{verbatim}
EvaluateHorizontalWallSegment(SourceID, BoundaryID)
EvaluateVerticalWallSegment(SourceID, BoundaryID)
EvaluateCDNozzleWallSegment(SourceID, BoundaryID)
\end{verbatim}

Horizontal marker segments classify targets as lower or upper wall markers.
Vertical marker segments classify targets as left or right wall markers.  In
both cases the evaluator constructs the stationary wall ghost for the mobile
source and returns normal, overlap area, center distance, and wall flag.  The
force accumulator then uses the configured contact-force measure.

\texttt{EvaluateCDNozzleWallSegment} is the current curved two-dimensional
nozzle evaluator.  Boundary markers still provide locality, but the wall point
and wall normal are calculated from the analytic CD nozzle radius profile at
the source particle's current axial coordinate.  This preserves the same
source-owned accumulation rule used by straight boundary-particle walls while
allowing the wall segment to be curved.

\ForceFunctionSection{BoundaryParticleAppliesToSource}

\texttt{BoundaryParticleAppliesToSource(SourceID, BoundaryID)} is the current
Python locality guard for boundary markers.  It approximates the GLSL
cell-list broad phase by accepting only markers near the source in \(x\) and
\(z\).  The GLSL boundary-particle path should receive marker candidates from
cell occupancy rather than from a full direct scan.

\ForceFunctionSection{IsBoundaryParticle}

\texttt{IsBoundaryParticle(ParticleID)} returns true when the particle record
has \texttt{ptype=1.0}.  Boundary particles are occupancy and wall-geometry
markers, not mobile dynamics sources.

\ForceFunctionSection{IsMobileParticleActiveForDynamics}

\texttt{IsMobileParticleActiveForDynamics(ParticleID)} combines reservoir
lifecycle state with particle type.  It is true only for live, non-boundary
particles.  Contact scans, velocity writes, position writes, diagnostics, and
reservoir birth/death updates use this mobile-source predicate.

\ForceFunctionSection{WallFlagsForDynamics}

\texttt{WallFlagsForDynamics()} selects the manual wall flags for the current
wall mode.  Ordinary simple boundaries use all four walls.  Reservoir pipe
flow uses only flags \(3\) and \(4\).  Boundary-particle wall mode bypasses
this manual wall scan because wall contacts are discovered as boundary-marker
targets.

\ForceFunctionSection{InitializeWallContactState}

\texttt{InitializeWallContactState(SourceID, wall)} represents a wall contact
with a stationary ghost.  Ordinary wall contacts use a ghost having the same
radius and mass as the source particle.  Starting wall contacts use an
effective ghost radius whose diameter equals the starting wall-ghost center
distance.  The contact normal is perpendicular to the wall.  The configurable
\texttt{wall\_contact\_offset} controls how far the physical ghost center lies
beyond the wall boundary.

\ForceFunctionSection{InitializeContactState}

\texttt{InitializeContactState(SourceID, TargetID)} obtains the current
particle-particle geometry and records one source-owned diagnostic contact
slot.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetContactState}

\texttt{GetContactState(SourceID, TargetID)} obtains a reusable current-frame
contact slot.  The slot contains geometry and diagnostic values only; it is
not used later to calculate force.

\ForceFunctionSection{AppendContactSlot}

\texttt{AppendContactSlot(SourceID, TargetID)} records an active
particle-contact slot owned by the source.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetContactSlots}

\texttt{GetContactSlots(SourceID)} returns the fixed diagnostic contact-slot
array owned by the source particle.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetParticleGeometry}

\texttt{GetParticleGeometry(SourceID, TargetID)} calculates overlap area,
center distance, penetration depth, and the source-to-target normal from the
immutable frame-start position snapshot.  In depth mode, the source orientation
vector points from the source center to the source boundary in the collision
direction, the proximity vector points from the source center to the target
leading edge, and penetration depth is the orientation magnitude minus the
proximity magnitude.  Ordinary contacts use the physical particle radii.  If
the pair belongs to a starting contact,
\texttt{GetParticleGeometry} obtains effective radii from
\texttt{GetParticleEffectiveContactGeometry}.  The effective radii make the
starting separation a zero-force limit.  If a resolved starting contact is
still physically overlapping, physical force is suppressed until the actual
radii separate.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetParticleEffectiveContactGeometry}

\texttt{GetParticleEffectiveContactGeometry(SourceID, TargetID,
center\_distance)} selects the radii used by both force and diagnostic
potential energy for one particle contact.  It returns physical radii for
ordinary contacts, effective radii for active starting contacts, and a
suppression flag for resolved starting contacts that have not yet cleared the
physical-radius overlap.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetParticlePosition}

\texttt{GetParticlePosition(ParticleID)} returns a particle position from the
selected immutable position snapshot.

\ForceFunctionSection{TunnelingDetection}

The current GLSL contact query in \texttt{isParticleContactMotion.glsl}
detects overlap or near-overlap at the sampled frame position:

\begin{verbatim}
float rddiff = dsq - rsq;
if (rddiff < 0.00001)
    return 1;
\end{verbatim}

This is an instantaneous overlap test.  It does not prove that the segment
traced from the previous position to the current position avoided the contact
radius.  A fast particle can therefore tunnel if it is outside the overlap
band at both sampled endpoints but crosses the band between them.

The planned tunneling error path should add a swept closest-approach test to
the hand-maintained contact-query shader infrastructure.  If the swept path
crosses the particle-particle contact radius, the shader should set the
compute error field and return a tunneling error rather than silently
continuing.  Boundary-particle wall tunneling requires the analogous test in
the wall-segment evaluator because the crossing is against a wall plane or
segment, not a particle center.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetStartFrameVelocity}

\texttt{GetStartFrameVelocity(ParticleID)} returns the immutable frame-start
velocity used by integration and relative-normal-velocity diagnostics.

\ForceFunctionSection{AccumulateContactForce}

For every current particle or wall contact,
\texttt{AccumulateContactForce(SourceID, contact\_state, totalForce)} applies:

\begin{equation}
F_{ij}=q_{ij}M_{ij},
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\mathbf F_i \mathrel{+}= -F_{ij}\mathbf n_{ij}.
\end{equation}

\(M_{ij}\) is selected by \texttt{contact\_force\_measure}.  In the default
\texttt{depth} mode, \(M_{ij}\) is penetration depth.  With
\(\mathbf O_i\) as the source orientation vector and \(\mathbf P_{ij}\) as the
source-to-target-leading-edge proximity vector:
\begin{equation}
M_{ij}=\delta_{ij}=\lVert\mathbf O_i\rVert-\lVert\mathbf P_{ij}\rVert,
\end{equation}
which reduces to:
\begin{equation}
M_{ij}=r_i+r_j-\lVert \mathbf x_j-\mathbf x_i\rVert.
\end{equation}
For wall contacts the equal-radius wall ghost gives
\(M_{ij}=2r_i-d_{\mathrm{wall}}\).  In \texttt{area} mode, \(M_{ij}\) is the
circle-overlap area \(A_{ij}\), preserving the earlier force law.  Python
continues to calculate overlap area in both modes for diagnostics and for
area-mode comparison.

The force is applied during both compression and rebound.  Starting contacts
use effective geometry measured from their starting separation.  There is no
contact weighting, available-momentum cap, or collision phase.  Source-owned
recoverable internal momentum is recorded as a diagnostic only and does not
feed back into velocity.

\ForceFunctionSection{RecoverableInternalMomentum}

Each source particle owns a recoverable internal momentum vector in
\texttt{parms.yzw}.  \texttt{parms.x} remains particle mass.  During
compression, \texttt{AccumulateContactForce} adds the current contact impulse
along the contact normal to this vector.  During rebound, it releases the
stored component along the same normal, limited by the current contact
impulse.  The scalar magnitude is mirrored to \texttt{Data.z} and to the
Python \texttt{internal\_momentum} report field.

This is diagnostic bookkeeping only.  It is intended to show recoverable
collision compression rising during approach, draining during rebound, and
remaining nonzero when crowding or confinement blocks release.  Heat,
thermalization, and heat-transfer coefficients are later model layers and are
not part of the current ForceDynamics update.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetContactStiffness}

\texttt{GetContactStiffness(SourceID, TargetID, contact\_type)} selects
particle-pair or wall stiffness for the current contact.

\ForceFunctionSection{GetPairStiffness}

\texttt{GetPairStiffness(SourceID, TargetID)} returns the nonnegative mean of
the two particle-owned stiffness values:

\begin{equation}
q_{ij}=\max\left(0,\frac{q_i+q_j}{2}\right).
\end{equation}

The units of \(q\) depend on \texttt{contact\_force\_measure}.  In
\texttt{depth} mode, \(q\) is depth stiffness.  In \texttt{area} mode, \(q\)
is area stiffness.

\ForceFunctionSection{CalcVelocity}

\texttt{CalcVelocity(SourceID, totalForce)} performs the source velocity
write.  It converts the completed source-local force sum to acceleration
inside the function:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf v_{n+1}
=
\mathbf v_n+\frac{\mathbf F_n}{m}\Delta t.
\end{equation}

\ForceFunctionSection{GetParticleMass}

\texttt{GetParticleMass(ParticleID)} returns particle mass from
\texttt{parms.x} with an epsilon lower bound.

\ForceFunctionSection{CalcPosition}

\texttt{CalcPosition(SourceID)} performs the semi-implicit Euler position
write using the newly calculated velocity:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf x_{n+1}
=
\mathbf x_n+\mathbf v_{n+1}\Delta t.
\end{equation}

This velocity-first update is the symplectic Euler method.  Symplectic Euler
does not conserve the system's exact total energy at every timestep.  Instead,
it typically produces a bounded energy excursion in which the calculated
total energy oscillates above and below the exact value.  Unlike standard
Euler integration, this error generally does not accumulate into continuous
energy growth or decay.  Decreasing the timestep reduces the excursion's
magnitude, making symplectic Euler well suited for long-running conservative
dynamics simulations.

\subsection{Potential Energy and Diagnostics}

The force is conservative with pair potential:

\begin{equation}
U_{ij}(d)=q_{ij}\int_d^{r_i+r_j}A_{ij}(s)\,ds.
\end{equation}

For a starting contact, \(r_i+r_j\) is replaced by the effective starting
separation limit while the contact is active.  After the contact rebounds to
that limit, force and potential energy remain suppressed until the actual
physical radii separate.  This avoids a second rebound caused only by the
initial configured overlap.

Potential energy, relative normal velocity, contact slots, end-position
snapshots, and pair-phase measurements are diagnostic only.  They do not
affect the source-local force sum or particle integration.  System energy is:

\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{total}}=KE+\sum_{i<j}U_{ij}+\sum_{i,w}U_{iw}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Current Numerical Results}

The central pair rule independently produces equal-and-opposite particle-pair
forces and preserves vector momentum.  Semi-implicit Euler introduces a small
bounded numerical energy excursion.  Current captures include:

\begin{verbatim}
ThreeParticle, 500 steps:
energy range = approximately [-9.18e-5, +9.98e-5]
final energy difference = approximately -1.69e-7

ParticlesInBox, 10000 steps:
energy range = approximately [-1.85e-5, +1.90e-5]
final energy difference = approximately -7.34e-7
\end{verbatim}

The long-running box result currently rises and falls within a bounded band
rather than showing monotonic energy drift.
