\section{Introduction}

Collision detection is a fundamental component of particle simulation,
computer graphics, and physics-based modeling. Applications such as
granular media, molecular dynamics, discrete element methods, and
particle-based fluid simulation may require the evaluation of millions
of particles within a single timestep. A naive all-pairs
collision-detection strategy exhibits quadratic complexity with respect
to particle count, resulting in approximately $(10^{12})$ pairwise
comparisons for a system containing one million particles. Consequently,
collision detection often becomes the dominant computational expense,
motivating the continued development of scalable broad-phase
collision-detection algorithms for large-scale simulation.

Existing broad-phase methods typically generate occupancy information
through dedicated collision-detection stages that remain separate from
the graphics pipeline. This work presents gPCD, a GPU-resident
collision-detection framework consisting of broad-phase and narrow-phase
stages. The primary contribution is a broad-phase formulation that
integrates occupancy generation directly into the graphics pipeline
through corner-based occupancy construction. Occupancy generation is
performed within the vertex stage during graphics-pipeline execution,
allowing broad-phase construction and rendering to proceed concurrently
on the GPU. A conventional narrow phase evaluates the resulting
collision candidates. Experimental results demonstrate efficient
processing of large particle counts, stable throughput-limited
operation, and near-linear scaling behavior.
