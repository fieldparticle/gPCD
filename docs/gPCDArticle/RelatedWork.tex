\section{Background and Related Work}

Numerous methodologies and execution architectures have been proposed
to improve collision-detection performance, particularly for large-scale
particle simulations. These approaches differ in the data structures
used to generate collision candidates, the execution architectures used
to process them, and their interaction with the rendering pipeline.
This section reviews representative collision-detection methodologies,
execution architectures, and rendering strategies, and identifies the
architectural gap addressed by the gPCD framework.

Most collision--detection frameworks separate candidate generation from
collision evaluation through broad-phase and narrow--phase stages. The
primary contribution of gPCD occurs during broad--phase occupancy
generation, while narrow-phase evaluation completes the collision--detection framework. 
Consequently, broad--phase methodologies play a
central role in determining the scalability of large-scale collision-
detection systems.

Numerous GPU-based broad-phase collision-detection approaches have been
proposed, including uniform grids \cite{barbieri2013}, spatial hashing
\cite{hastings}, hierarchical spatial subdivision structures
\cite{Evangelou2023}, sweep and prune \cite{capannini2016}, image-space
methods \cite{Faure2008,Jang2008a}, and other acceleration techniques
\cite{benitez2005,jansen2018}. These techniques differ in how spatial
information is organized and queried, but all seek to reduce the number
of pairwise evaluations passed to the narrow phase. As particle counts
and GPU capabilities have increased, research has expanded beyond
broad-phase methodologies to consider the execution architectures used
to implement them.


Collision-detection methodologies have been implemented using a wide
range of execution architectures. Early systems performed both
broad-phase and narrow-phase operations on the CPU. As particle counts
increased and graphics hardware became increasingly programmable,
portions of the collision-detection pipeline were migrated to the GPU
to exploit massive parallelism \cite{brodtkorb2013}. These developments
produced a spectrum of architectures ranging from CPU-resident
implementations to GPU-assisted and fully GPU-resident approaches.

In CPU-resident architectures, both broad-phase and narrow-phase
operations are performed on the CPU. GPU-assisted architectures offload
selected collision-detection operations to the GPU while maintaining
CPU-managed coordination and data structures. GPU-resident
architectures further reduce data movement by performing both
broad-phase and narrow-phase operations on the GPU, improving parallel
utilization and minimizing host-device communication. Although these architectures differ 
in execution location and data movement, they do not fundamentally
alter the rendering scheme.

Collision detection and rendering have historically served distinct
roles within simulation systems. Collision detection identifies particle
interactions and advances simulation state, whereas rendering
visualizes the resulting geometry and therefore acts primarily as a
consumer of simulation state.

This separation has persisted as simulation systems have evolved.
Although both rendering and collision detection increasingly leverage
GPU parallelism, even GPU-resident implementations
\cite{he2018,Harada2007} typically maintain a logical separation between
the two. Collision-detection operations are performed using dedicated
computational stages, while rendering serves primarily as a
visualization process. Consequently, rendering contributes little or no
information to collision detection.

To reduce this separation, several approaches have explored tighter
integration between rendering and simulation through image-space
techniques, GPU data structures, and graphics-assisted computation
\cite{Faure2008}. Nevertheless, occupancy generation and candidate
construction generally remain dedicated collision-detection operations
performed independently of rasterization. As a result, rendering
remains largely downstream of simulation and collision-detection
processes rather than serving as an active participant in broad-phase
collision detection. gPCD is intended to close this remaining architectural gap.
