\section{Experimental Results}

This section presents the measured performance of the gPCD framework
using randomly distributed particle configurations of increasing size.
Unless otherwise noted, benchmark cases maintain an approximately
constant collision fraction while varying the total particle count.
Performance is evaluated using throughput, time-per-particle, and
scaling analyses to characterize the behavior of the framework across
the tested particle range.

\subsection{Memory Locality Benchmark}

This benchmark evaluates the sensitivity of the gPCD framework to
memory locality. Two occupancy configurations were considered. In the
first configuration, cell occupancy data remained sequentially ordered
within memory. In the second configuration, occupancy data were
randomly distributed throughout the cell array.

Figure~\ref{fig:A_PQBS_V_PQBR} compares the resulting execution times as a
function of particle count. Sequential ordering consistently produced
lower execution times throughout the evaluated benchmark range. The
largest benchmark configuration required approximately
\nc{Sequential Largest Time} ms for sequential ordering and
\nc{Random Largest Time} ms for random ordering.

Although both configurations exhibit similar scaling behavior, random
ordering introduces a measurable performance penalty. The results
indicate that memory locality remains an important contributor to
overall performance despite the GPU-resident nature of the framework.

\subsection{Throughput and Time-per-Particle Behavior}

Throughput and time-per-particle provide complementary views of
framework performance. Throughput measures the aggregate rate at which
particles are processed, whereas time-per-particle normalizes execution
cost by particle count and therefore provides a direct measure of
scaling efficiency.

Figure~\ref{fig:A_PQBR_THROUGHPUT_AND_TPP} presents complementary
throughput and time-per-particle views of framework performance across
the evaluated particle range.

The throughput curve exhibits three distinct operating regions.
At low particle counts, throughput increases rapidly with increasing
particle count. A peak throughput region is subsequently observed,
followed by a throughput-limited region at larger benchmark sizes.

The utilization region extends approximately to
\numprint{10000} particles, after which the
framework enters a peak throughput region. Beyond
\numprint{2000000} particles, throughput becomes
throughput-limited.

\subsection{Scaling Analysis}
\input{A_PQBR_LOGLOG_REGRESS}
Log--log regression was performed to quantify the scaling behavior of
graphics, compute, and total execution times. A power-law model of the
form
\begin{equation}
t = aN^b
\end{equation}
was fitted to the measured data, where $t$ is execution time, $N$ is
particle count, $a$ is a proportionality constant, and $b$ is the
scaling exponent.

Figure~\ref{fig:A_PQBR_LOGLOG_REGRESS} presents the resulting log--log
regressions for graphics, compute, and total execution times. The
measured scaling exponents were found to be
$b_g=\nc{Graphics Slope}$,
$b_c=\nc{Compute Slope}$, and
$b_t=\nc{Total Slope}$.
All three exponents remain close to unity, indicating that execution
time increases approximately linearly with particle count throughout
the evaluated benchmark range.

The measured scaling exponents remain close to unity for graphics,
compute, and total execution times, providing experimental support for
the near-linear scaling behavior predicted by the complexity analysis.

\subsection{Curvature Analysis}
\input{A_PBQR_CURVATURE}
While log--log regression quantifies the overall scaling exponent,
curvature analysis provides a complementary measure of deviation from
ideal linear behavior. Quadratic fits were therefore applied to the
graphics, compute, and total execution-time measurements over the
evaluated benchmark range.

Figure~\ref{fig:A_PBQR_CURVATURE} presents the resulting quadratic fits and
associated curvature bounds. In all cases, the fitted curves remain
close to the measured data, indicating only minor departures from
linear behavior. The graphics and total execution times exhibit
particularly small curvature, while the compute measurements display
slightly larger but still bounded deviations.

The measured curvature bounds remain small relative to the overall
execution-time trends. Combined with the log--log scaling exponents,
these results provide additional evidence that the gPCD framework
maintains near-linear scaling throughout the evaluated benchmark range.
