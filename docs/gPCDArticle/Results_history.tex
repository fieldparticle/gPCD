\section{Results and Discussion} \label{results}


The performance parameters generated by the various tests discussed provide the information required to \textit{tune} the system with respect to the CPU-GPU combination or particle application - to include the addition of dynamics. Interrelated examinations of the data emphasize different criteria so that the user can determine how changes to the software or hardware impact the parallel algorithm. These criteria can then be used to determined the optimal operating configuration. The information needed includes throughput, per-particle cost, curvature, linearity, saturation point, complexity exponent, and throughput limited range. These suffice as tuning parameters without the need to evaluate GPU internals.

\subsubsection{Throughput and Time per particle}

The results of throughput and time–per–particle studies indicate that the GPU does not reach a stable fully occupied regime until problem sizes exceed approximately $N \!\approx\! 10^{6}$ particles.


For smaller workloads, fixed overheads such as kernel launch latency, command submission, and synchronization dominate the measured time~\cite{zhang2019overheads, chen2015freelaunch}. Insufficient parallelism leads to under-occupancy of streaming multiprocessors (SMs), preventing full latency hiding. Occupancy and warp execution efficiency increase only once enough parallel work is issued to saturate the hardware~\cite{nvidia2020occupancy,GPUOccupancy}. Together, these effects explain the inflated per-particle cost observed below $10^{6}$ particles. As $N$ increases further to $N=$\numprint{\APQBRTIMEPERPALLstartval{}}, the system transitions into a throughput-limited regime, where sustained memory bandwidth and fixed-function throughput bound performance~\cite{lustig2013reducing}.

\subsubsection{Curvature}

The quadratic fit $T(N) = aN^2 + bN + c$ shows the trend between frame time and number of particles ($N$). The the quadratic component, $a$, is \APBQRCURVATUREgmsa{}s for graphics, \APBQRCURVATUREcmsa{}s for compute, and for a total of \APBQRCURVATUREtota{}s. 

The compute pipeline yields a negative coefficient in the saturated range implying that the curvature term bends downward rather than upward. This may indicate that in the throughput limited range the 
compute pipeline is still gaining efficiency. 

\input {inc/A_PQBR_THROUGHPUT_AND_TPP.tex}

The curvature, $\kappa$ is the second derivative of the quadratic fit line ($2a \left[ \frac{s}{N^2} \right]$). This is multiplied by the maximum number of particles to produce quadratic contribution to per-particle cost, $\Delta t_n \left[ \frac{s}{N} \right]$. The per-particle cost is again multiplied by the number of particles to give the total quadratic contribution $\triangle T \left[s \right]$ in seconds. The total quadratic contribution divided by the maximum time, at the maximum number of particles, gives an estimate of how much the curvature is impacting the frame time. Multiplied by 100 the percentage impact is \APBQRCURVATUREgmsQuadraticContributionpct{}\% for graphics \APBQRCURVATUREcmsQuadraticContributionpct{}\% for compute. A full listing is provided in Supplement Information, Sec. \ref{suppliment}.

\subsubsection{Log--Log Regression Results}

A separate study of the log--log regressions was performed for graphics time ($t_G$), compute time ($t_C$), and total frame time ($t_T = t_G + t_C$) versus particle count $N$. Each regression was fit both across the full data range and in the saturated regime ($N \geq 10^{6}$). The saturated fits yielded slopes of $b \approx 1.0$ with coefficients of determination $R^{2} > 0.999$ across all three cases, \figo{A_PQBR_LOGLOG_REGRESS}. 

In addition to previous analysis, this demonstrates that both the graphics and compute pipelines scale linearly with particle count once the GPU achieves full occupancy. The empirical slopes show no evidence of superlinear behavior in either the graphics or compute paths. Instead, both regressions flatten to a slope of approximately one in the saturated regime, and the combined total time follows the same linear trend. 

\subsection{Collision Fraction Benchmark (CFB)}\label{cfb}

\Figo{A_CFB} reports the compute and graphics execution times for various collision fraction percentages. CFB-R tests a constant \numprint{\ACFBN{}} particles over a collision fraction that varied from 0.0 to 1.0 in 0.05 increments, at 8 particles per cell. This graph indicates that the graphics pipeline is insensitive to the number of collisions - which is expected as this pipeline only places particle locations. The compute pipeline slows slightly as the number of collisions increase but also appears to be impacted little by increasing collisions.
